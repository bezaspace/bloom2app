# Practitioner-Designed Tracking Plans

> **Purpose of this document:** This is a self-contained specification for the
> next major feature set of Bloom2. It is written so that an AI coding agent
> in a fresh session can read it and implement the feature without any other
> context. It covers the *what*, the *why*, the *how*, and — critically — the
> *why we chose this approach over alternatives* for every major decision.
>
> **Status:** Not yet implemented. This is the plan.
>
> **Date written:** 2026-07-02

---

## 1. What we are building

### The problem with the current app

Today, every patient tracks the same six hardcoded wellness domains:
`workout`, `diet`, `medication`, `mental_health`, `meditation`, `other`.
These are defined as a constant (`WELLNESS_DOMAINS`) in
`backend/app/dashboard/schemas.py` and mirrored in
`frontend/src/dashboard.ts`. The 90-day plan (generated by Bloom during
voice onboarding) has phases and actions, but the *tracking* layer is
identical for everyone — a 25-year-old athlete and a 60-year-old with
hypertension see the same dashboard cards.

This is a consumer wellness app pattern. It does not match how preventive
medicine actually works.

### What we are building instead

The practitioner **designs a tracking plan per patient** — selecting which
metrics to track, their targets, their units, their frequency, and which
biomarker outcomes the plan is aiming for. The patient's mobile dashboard,
Bloom's voice check-ins, the practitioner's analytics view, and the AI
insights all read from this plan. No more hardcoded `WELLNESS_DOMAINS`.

**Example:** Dr. Sharma (endocrinologist) designs this plan for a patient
with prediabetes:

- **Outcome target:** HbA1c < 6.0% within 90 days (current: 6.1%, from labs)
- **Outcome target:** Fasting glucose < 100 mg/dL within 90 days (current: 108)
- **Tracked metric:** Fasting glucose — daily, morning, manual entry, target < 100 mg/dL
- **Tracked metric:** Steps — daily, target 8,000
- **Tracked metric:** Sleep duration — nightly, target ≥ 7 hrs
- **Tracked metric:** Meditation — daily 15 min (stress → glucose)
- **Tracked metric:** Carbs per meal — log each meal, target < 45g
- **Phase 1 (days 1-30):** Focus on building sleep + meditation habits; track steps but don't stress about target.
- **Phase 2 (days 31-60):** Add fasting glucose tracking; increase step target to 10,000.
- **Phase 3 (days 61-90):** Full plan; reassess HbA1c via new lab.

Meanwhile Dr. Chen (mental health) designs a completely different plan for
another patient: mood 3x/day, anxiety severity 1-5, sleep, therapy homework
completion, medication timing — no biomarkers at all. And a nutritionist
might focus on macros, hydration, and weight trend.

### The core conceptual frame: Outcomes vs. Levers

Every plan has two tiers of metrics:

- **Outcome targets** — the biomarker goals (HbA1c < 6.0, LDL < 100,
  Vitamin D > 30). These come from lab data, are measured infrequently
  (weeks/months), and are the **why** — the clinical reason the plan exists.
- **Behavioral levers** — the daily/weekly tracked metrics (steps, sleep,
  meditation, fasting glucose, meds). These are measured frequently
  (daily/weekly) and are the **how** — the lifestyle changes expected to
  move the outcomes.

The AI's most important job is to **connect them**: "your behavioral
adherence is 85% but your fasting glucose isn't improving — the lever may
not be the right one; consider discussing with your practitioner." This is
what makes the insights clinically meaningful rather than just "you hit 80%
of your targets."

## 2. Why we are building this

1. **It matches how preventive medicine actually works.** The core loop of
   preventive medicine is: *prescribe a lifestyle intervention → track
   adherence → measure biomarker outcomes → adjust the intervention*. A
   fixed 6-domain template doesn't model this loop. A practitioner-designed
   plan with outcome targets + behavioral levers does.

2. **It makes the practitioner app indispensable.** Today the practitioner
   app is read-only — it shows data the patient already tracks. If the
   practitioner *designs* what gets tracked, the practitioner app becomes
   the control center. That's a much stronger reason for a practitioner to
   log in and engage.

3. **It gives the AI something concrete to reason about.** Today Bloom's
   insights are generic ("you meditated 3 days in a row"). If the plan says
   "target HbA1c < 6.0 via daily fasting glucose < 100 + 8k steps + 7hr
   sleep," then the AI can generate *targeted* insights grounded in the
   specific clinical goal: "You're 2 weeks in; fasting glucose is averaging
   108, above target. Your step count is on target but sleep is averaging
   6.2 hrs — that's the likely driver. Focus on sleep this week."

4. **It differentiates us from every generic wellness app.** "Your doctor
   designed this tracking plan for you, based on your labs" is a
   fundamentally different value proposition than "track your steps."

5. **Bloom2's stated mission is preventive medicine via lifestyle
   optimization for biomarker improvement.** The tracking plan is the
   embodiment of that mission — it's the prescription, the adherence
   monitor, and the outcome tracker in one structure.

## 3. Key decisions and their rationale

### Decision 1: Structured plan builder, NOT free-form custom metrics (for v1)

**Choice:** The practitioner picks metrics from a **library of pre-defined
metric templates** (fasting glucose, steps, sleep duration, mood,
meditation minutes, weight, blood pressure, heart rate, macros, hydration,
specific medications, specific symptoms, etc.) and configures each one's
target / frequency / unit. Free-form custom metrics are a future feature.

**Why not free-form from day one:** A blank canvas ("design any metric")
sounds flexible but in practice leads to:
- Practitioners don't know what to define → low engagement.
- Patients get inconsistent experiences across practitioners.
- The data shapes are too varied for analytics and AI to reason about
  (you can't compute "average fasting glucose vs target" if every
  practitioner defines glucose differently).
- Chart selection becomes impossible (what chart do you draw for a
  free-form metric?).

A template library keeps the data clean enough for analytics and AI while
still giving the practitioner meaningful configuration freedom (target,
frequency, unit, time-of-day, phase emphasis). Custom metrics can be added
later once the structured foundation is proven.

### Decision 2: The tracking plan and the 90-day plan MERGE into one structure

**Choice:** The current `plan_json` (phases/actions/weekly_rhythm, generated
by Bloom) and the current 6 hardcoded wellness domains are replaced by a
single **Plan** structure that contains: outcome targets, tracked metrics,
and phase structure. One source of truth.

**Why not keep them separate:** Having three overlapping concepts (Bloom's
plan, the practitioner's tracking plan, the hardcoded domains) creates
confusion about which is authoritative and leads to inconsistent UI. The
plan *is* the set of tracked metrics + targets + phases + biomarker
outcomes. Bloom's onboarding-generated plan becomes the **default** plan
(created from the same templates); the practitioner can then override or
refine it. There is exactly one active plan per patient at any time.

### Decision 3: Visuals are auto-generated from the plan, NOT separately designed

**Choice:** The practitioner designs *what to track and target*; the system
auto-generates *how to show it*. Each metric template type has a default
visualization (line chart for trends, bar chart for daily counts, ring for
adherence %, gauge for biomarker-vs-target). The patient dashboard and
practitioner analytics view render whatever charts the plan implies.

**Why not let the practitioner pick charts:** It's extra burden on the
practitioner (they're clinicians, not dashboard designers), it leads to
inconsistent UX for patients across practitioners, and the "right" chart
is almost always determined by the data type anyway. The system should be
opinionated about visualization.

### Decision 4: The practitioner has final authority on the active plan

**Choice:** The practitioner can edit the plan at any time. Bloom (the AI)
can *suggest* plan adjustments (surfaced to the practitioner for approval
in the web app). The patient can log data and view the plan but cannot
redefine targets.

**Why not let the patient edit:** This preserves the clinical chain of
authority. The practitioner is the clinician; the plan is the prescription.
If the patient could change their own targets, the adherence data becomes
meaningless. Bloom suggesting (but not auto-applying) changes gives the AI
a voice without overriding clinical authority.

**Why not let Bloom auto-apply changes:** For the same reason — Bloom is not
a licensed clinician. Suggestions go to the practitioner for a one-click
approve/edit/reject.

### Decision 5: Soft cap of ~7 active daily metrics

**Choice:** The plan builder gently discourages more than 7 daily tracked
metrics. The AI flags "this plan may be too demanding" if the practitioner
exceeds it. It's a soft cap, not a hard limit.

**Why:** If a practitioner defines 15 metrics to track daily, the patient
will churn. Preventive medicine only works if the patient actually does it.
Focus is a feature.

### Decision 6: New `metric_id` column replaces `domain` in daily_logs

**Choice:** The `daily_logs` table's `domain` column (currently one of the 6
hardcoded strings) is replaced by a `metric_id` foreign key to a
`plan_metrics` table. Each tracked metric has a stable ID, so logs are
always attributable to the right metric even if the plan changes.

**Why not keep `domain` as a free-text field:** A foreign key gives us
referential integrity, lets us join logs to metric definitions (for target
comparison, charting, adherence scoring), and survives plan edits (if a
practitioner changes a target mid-plan, old logs still reference the
metric). A free-text `domain` would degrade into inconsistency.

**Migration:** Existing `daily_logs` rows (with domain values like
"workout", "meditation") are migrated to the new schema by creating a
default plan for each existing patient with metrics matching the old 6
domains, then re-pointing the old log rows to the new metric IDs. See
section 9 (Migration strategy).

### Decision 7: Adherence is computed, not stored

**Choice:** Adherence percentages (per-metric, overall) are computed
on-the-fly from logs + plan targets, not stored as a separate table. They
are cached in the schedule cache (which already exists per-day) if
performance requires it.

**Why not store adherence:** Stored adherence would need to be recomputed
every time a log is added, a target is changed, or a plan is edited.
Computing it on-the-fly from logs + targets is simpler and always accurate.
The daily schedule cache (which already exists) can hold the day's
adherence snapshot if raw computation is too slow.

### Decision 8: Insights are generated on-demand, not pre-computed

**Choice:** AI insights (correlations, weekly reports, trend alerts) are
generated on-demand via Gemini Flash-Lite calls, similar to the existing
`practitioner_ai.py` pattern. They are cached where it makes sense (e.g.,
weekly report cached for the week; correlation analysis cached for the day)
but the source of truth is always the live data + the plan.

**Why not a background insight engine:** A background job that continuously
analyzes data adds infrastructure complexity (a task queue, a scheduler)
that isn't justified at this stage. On-demand generation with caching is
simpler and sufficient. A background engine can be added later if the
on-demand approach proves too slow or too expensive in API calls.

### Decision 9: Hybrid analytics — manual computation + AI interpretation

**Choice:** Analytics are split into two layers:

- **Layer 1 (computation):** `plan_analytics.py` — manual Python functions
  that compute adherence %, Pearson correlation coefficients, trend slopes,
  and outcome progress from logs + plan + biomarkers. Deterministic,
  testable, instant, free.
- **Layer 2 (interpretation):** `plan_insights.py` — Gemini Flash-Lite
  takes the computed numbers + raw context (patient notes, plan rationale,
  biomarker history) and generates narrative insights, clinical reasoning,
  and plan adjustment suggestions.

The LLM never does the math. It receives the math already done and focuses
on what it's actually good at: making sense of the numbers, connecting them
to clinical knowledge, and expressing it in language a human can act on.

**Why not let the AI do all the analysis ( computation + interpretation):**

1. **Hallucination risk** — This is the dealbreaker for health data. If you
   ask an LLM "what's the correlation between sleep and mood?", it will
   sometimes get it right, sometimes be approximately right (r = -0.52 when
   the real answer is -0.61), sometimes hallucinate a correlation that
   doesn't exist, and sometimes miss a real one. A practitioner adjusting a
   care plan based on a hallucinated correlation is a real clinical problem.
   The math here is simple (Pearson, percentages, slopes) and should be done
   by deterministic code.
2. **Reproducibility** — Manual functions are deterministic: same input,
   same output, every time. LLM outputs vary between calls. "The app told
   me different things on different days" is a credibility killer for a
   health product.
3. **Verifiability** — With manual functions you can write a unit test:
   "given this log data, adherence should be 87.3%." You can't unit-test
   an LLM's analysis. For a health product, you want to prove the numbers.
4. **Cost and latency** — Feeding 90 days × 7 metrics into an LLM prompt
   on every page load is expensive and slow. Manual computation is instant
   and free. The LLM should be called only for interpretation, and cached.
5. **The UI needs actual numbers** — Charts, adherence rings, correlation
   coefficients, trend arrows need real numbers to render. You can't render
   a chart from LLM prose. So you need the computed numbers regardless.

**Where AI-only IS the right call (and the plan uses it):**

- **Qualitative analysis** — extracting themes from patient free-text notes
  ("felt anxious all day, skipped lunch"). No manual function can do this.
- **Non-obvious pattern discovery** — "your mood dips every other Tuesday,
  aligning with bi-weekly team meetings." Requires reasoning about context,
  not just math. An LLM reading raw data + notes might surface this.
- **Natural language generation** — turning "r = -0.61, n = 18" into "I
  noticed your mood is lower on days you sleep less than 6 hours."
- **Clinical reasoning** — "sleep affects insulin sensitivity, so if
  fasting glucose is rising despite good diet adherence, check sleep first."

The hybrid gives you trustworthy numbers + nuanced interpretation, which is
better than either approach alone.

### Decision 10: AI-assisted plan creation via ADK agent + manual fallback

**Choice:** The practitioner can create and edit a patient's tracking plan
in two modes, switchable via a toggle in the UI:

1. **AI Chat mode (primary):** A conversational interface powered by an ADK
   agent ("Plan Designer") that has tools to read the patient's data
   (biomarkers, profile, doc summary, existing plan, adherence analytics)
   and to modify a plan draft (add/remove metrics, set targets, define
   phases, set outcomes, write rationale). The practitioner talks to the AI
   in natural language: "Create a prediabetes reversal plan for this
   patient" → the AI proposes a complete plan with reasoning; the
   practitioner refines: "lower the step target, they're 62 and sedentary"
   → the AI adjusts and explains why 7,000 might be better than 10,000.
   The AI proactively suggests: "I notice you didn't include sleep tracking
   — given this patient's HbA1c, sleep is a key lever. Want to add it?"
2. **Manual mode (fallback):** The form-based PlanBuilder UI where the
   practitioner selects metrics from the template library, configures
   targets/frequencies/phases, and saves. No AI involvement.

Both modes edit the **same plan draft**, which persists in the database
(not in ADK session state). The practitioner can switch between modes
mid-edit: start in AI chat, switch to manual to tweak one number, switch
back to AI to ask for a suggestion. The draft is the single source of
truth; the two modes are just different ways to modify it.

**Why an ADK agent (not a single structured-output call):**

Plan creation is not a one-shot generation. It's an **iterative,
conversational design process** where:
- The AI proactively suggests ("you didn't include sleep tracking...")
- The practitioner responds ("good idea, add it with a 7hr target")
- The AI adjusts and explains ("done — I set 7hrs because...")
- The practitioner follows up ("actually make the step target lower")
- The AI adjusts again ("lowered to 7,000. At 62 and sedentary, starting
  lower and ramping up in phase 2 is safer...")

This multi-turn loop with tool calls, observation, and adjustment is
exactly what ADK agents are designed for. A single structured-output call
can produce a first draft but can't handle the iterative refinement.

**Why a manual fallback (not AI-only):**

1. Some plans are simple enough that manual is faster — a practitioner who
   knows exactly what they want shouldn't have to chat with an AI to set 3
   metrics.
2. Some practitioners will prefer manual control, especially early in
   adoption when they're calibrating trust in the AI's suggestions.
3. The manual mode is also the "escape hatch" if the AI suggests something
   the practitioner disagrees with — they can switch to manual and override.
4. The manual form is needed anyway for quick edits ("just change this one
   target") where a full conversation would be overkill.

**Why both modes edit the same draft:**

If the AI chat and manual form edited separate data, the practitioner would
lose work when switching modes. By sharing a single DB-backed draft, the
practitioner can fluidly switch between AI assistance and manual control
without losing context. The draft is committed to an active plan (a new
version) only when the practitioner clicks "Publish" — until then it's a
work-in-progress draft.

**Why the AI uses tools (not just text generation):**

The ADK agent's tools (`add_metric`, `set_target`, `define_phase`, etc.)
write directly to the plan draft in the database. This means:
- The plan draft is always in a valid structured state (not free text that
  needs parsing).
- The manual mode can render the current draft at any time (it reads from
  the same DB rows the AI tools wrote to).
- The AI's changes are immediately visible if the practitioner switches to
  manual mode mid-conversation.
- The AI can read back the current draft state (via a `get_draft` tool) to
  stay grounded in what's already been added.

**Why not let the AI auto-publish the plan:**

Same reasoning as Decision 4 — the practitioner has clinical authority. The
AI modifies a *draft*, not the active plan. The practitioner reviews the
draft (in either mode) and clicks "Publish" to make it the active plan. This
preserves the human-in-the-loop checkpoint.

## 4. Data model

### New tables

```sql
-- A tracking plan for a patient. One active plan per patient at a time.
-- Replaces the current user_profiles.plan_json (which held phases/actions/
-- weekly_rhythm as a blob). The plan_json blob is kept for backward compat
-- during migration but the new tables become the source of truth.
CREATE TABLE plans (
    id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT,
    patient_username TEXT NOT NULL,
    practitioner_id INTEGER,          -- NULL if plan was auto-generated by Bloom during onboarding
    version INTEGER NOT NULL DEFAULT 1, -- incremented on each edit
    is_active INTEGER NOT NULL DEFAULT 1,
    title TEXT,                        -- e.g., "Prediabetes reversal — 90 days"
    rationale TEXT,                    -- practitioner's free-text reasoning (visible to patient + AI)
    created_at TEXT NOT NULL,
    updated_at TEXT NOT NULL
);

-- Outcome targets: the biomarker goals the plan is aiming for.
CREATE TABLE plan_outcomes (
    id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT,
    plan_id INTEGER NOT NULL,
    biomarker_name TEXT NOT NULL,      -- e.g., "HbA1c", "LDL Cholesterol"
    target_value REAL NOT NULL,
    target_direction TEXT NOT NULL,    -- "below" | "above" | "range"
    target_high REAL,                  -- used when direction = "range"
    unit TEXT NOT NULL,                -- e.g., "%", "mg/dL"
    target_date TEXT,                  -- ISO date by which to achieve it
    current_value REAL,                -- latest known value (synced from biomarkers table)
    current_as_of TEXT,                -- date of current_value reading
    FOREIGN KEY (plan_id) REFERENCES plans(id)
);

-- Tracked metrics: the behavioral levers the patient logs against.
-- Each metric is an instance of a template (see METRIC_TEMPLATES below).
CREATE TABLE plan_metrics (
    id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT,
    plan_id INTEGER NOT NULL,
    template_id TEXT NOT NULL,         -- FK to METRIC_TEMPLATES key, e.g., "fasting_glucose"
    label TEXT NOT NULL,               -- display name, e.g., "Fasting Glucose" (from template, editable)
    unit TEXT NOT NULL,                -- from template, editable
    frequency TEXT NOT NULL,           -- "daily" | "weekly" | "per_meal" | "as_needed"
    time_of_day TEXT,                  -- "morning" | "afternoon" | "evening" | "night" | NULL
    target_type TEXT NOT NULL,         -- "minimum" | "maximum" | "range" | "exact" | "count"
    target_value REAL,                 -- the target (interpretation depends on target_type)
    target_high REAL,                  -- upper bound when target_type = "range"
    is_active INTEGER NOT NULL DEFAULT 1, -- can be deactivated without deleting (for phase transitions)
    phase INT,                         -- which plan phase (1, 2, 3) this metric is emphasized in; NULL = all phases
    sort_order INTEGER NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,
    FOREIGN KEY (plan_id) REFERENCES plans(id)
);

-- Phases of the plan (replaces plan_json.phases).
CREATE TABLE plan_phases (
    id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT,
    plan_id INTEGER NOT NULL,
    phase_number INTEGER NOT NULL,     -- 1, 2, 3
    name TEXT NOT NULL,                -- "Phase 1: Days 1-30"
    focus TEXT,                        -- what to focus on in this phase
    actions TEXT,                      -- JSON array of action strings
    day_start INTEGER NOT NULL,        -- 1, 31, 61
    day_end INTEGER NOT NULL,          -- 30, 60, 90
    FOREIGN KEY (plan_id) REFERENCES plans(id)
);

-- Plan drafts: a work-in-progress plan that hasn't been published yet.
-- Both the AI chat mode and the manual mode edit this draft. When the
-- practitioner clicks "Publish", the draft is copied into a new active
-- plan (a new row in `plans` + child tables) and the draft is marked
-- published. Until then, the draft is editable in either mode.
-- At most one unpublished draft per patient at a time.
CREATE TABLE plan_drafts (
    id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT,
    patient_username TEXT NOT NULL,
    practitioner_id INTEGER NOT NULL,
    title TEXT,
    rationale TEXT,
    -- The draft stores its metrics, outcomes, and phases as JSON blobs
    -- (not in separate child tables) because it's a transient working
    -- copy. On publish, these are exploded into plan_metrics, plan_outcomes,
    -- plan_phases rows.
    outcomes_json TEXT,        -- JSON array of outcome target objects
    metrics_json TEXT,         -- JSON array of metric objects
    phases_json TEXT,          -- JSON array of phase objects
    is_published INTEGER NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,
    created_at TEXT NOT NULL,
    updated_at TEXT NOT NULL
);
```

shapes mirror the child tables but are flexible (no referential integrity
needed for a draft):

### Adherence computation (`plan_analytics.py`)

For each active metric on a given date, adherence is:

- **`minimum` target:** `min(1.0, actual / target)` — e.g., 7,500 steps / 8,000 target = 93.8%
- **`maximum` target:** `1.0 if actual <= target else (target / actual)` — e.g., fasting glucose 108 vs target 100 = 92.6% (worse the more you exceed)
- **`range` target:** `1.0 if target_low <= actual <= target_high else 0.0` — binary
- **`count` target:** `min(1.0, actual / target)` — e.g., 2 meals / 3 target = 66.7%
- **`exact` target:** `1.0 if actual == target else 0.0`
- **`none` target (symptom tracking):** no adherence — tracked for pattern detection only

**Overall adherence score** = weighted average of all active metrics'
adherence, weighted by `1.0` for daily-frequency metrics and `0.5` for
weekly/as-needed metrics. Capped at 0-100%. Metrics with `target_type=none`
are excluded from the overall score.

### Correlation analysis (`plan_analytics.py`)

For patients with ≥ 14 days of data on ≥ 2 metrics, compute pairwise
Pearson correlation coefficients between daily-aggregated metric values.
Surface pairs with |r| ≥ 0.4 (moderate correlation) and ≥ 14 overlapping
data points. Present as: "On days you sleep <6 hrs, your mood averages 2.3
vs 3.8 on 7+ hr days (r = -0.61, n = 18 days)."

This is a simple statistical correlation, not causal inference. The AI
insights layer adds the clinical interpretation.

#### Agent instruction (summary)

The agent is instructed to:
1. Act as a clinical assistant helping a practitioner design a tracking plan.
2. Always read the patient's data (biomarkers, profile, doc summary) before
   proposing metrics — never suggest metrics blind.
3. Propose outcome targets derived from the patient's actual biomarker
   values (e.g., if HbA1c is 6.1, suggest target < 6.0).
4. Select 4-7 tracked metrics from the template library that are clinically
   relevant to the patient's conditions and outcome targets.
5. Explain the reasoning for each metric and target ("I'm including sleep
   because sleep deprivation worsens insulin sensitivity, which is the
   driver of this patient's prediabetes").
6. Proactively suggest additions/changes ("I notice you haven't included
   sleep tracking — given this patient's HbA1c, sleep is a key lever. Want
   to add it?").
7. When the practitioner asks to change something, make the change via tools
   AND explain the clinical reasoning.
8. Never publish the plan — that's the practitioner's action. The agent
   only modifies the draft.
9. When editing an existing plan, first read the current plan + adherence
   analytics to understand what's working and what isn't before suggesting
   changes.
10. Respect the soft cap of ~7 daily metrics — warn the practitioner if the
    draft exceeds it.

**Read tools (no side effects):**

| `get_patient_doc_summary(patient_username)` | Extracted conditions, medications, allergies from health docs |
| `get_current_plan(patient_username)` | The current active plan (if any) — metrics, outcomes, phases, adherence |

| Tool | Effect |

| `set_metric_target(patient_username, temp_id, target_value, target_high)` | Updates a metric's target in the draft. |

| `remove_outcome_from_draft(patient_username, index)` | Removes an outcome by index. |

returns the updated draft (so the agent sees the result of its action).

#### Runner bridge (`runner.py`)

The plan design agent runs via ADK's `Runner` with a text-based (non-live)
model. The bridge between the FastAPI endpoint and the Runner:

1. **`start_session(patient_username, practitioner_id)`** — creates a new
   ADK Runner session, creates a draft if none exists, returns `session_id`.
2. **`send_message(session_id, message)`** — sends the practitioner's text
   to the Runner via `runner.run_async()`, streams events back as SSE.
   Each event is either:
   - `{"type": "text", "content": "..."}` — agent's text response
   - `{"type": "tool_call", "tool": "add_metric_to_draft", "result": {...}}` —
     a tool was called (the web app can show "AI added: Fasting Glucose" in
     the chat as a system message)
   - `{"type": "done"}` — the agent finished its turn
3. **`get_history(session_id)`** — returns the conversation history for
   session restore.

The SSE stream is consumed by the web app's `PlanDesignChat` client
component (see section 7). The agent's tool calls modify the draft in the
DB in real-time, so if the practitioner switches to manual mode mid-chat,
the form reflects the AI's changes immediately.

#### Why SSE (not WebSocket)?

The plan design agent is a request-response conversation (practitioner sends
a message, agent responds with a stream of events, then waits for the next
message). SSE is simpler than WebSocket for this pattern — unidirectional
server→client streaming, no need for bidirectional binary frames. The
existing WebSocket infrastructure is reserved for Bloom's live audio (which
is genuinely bidirectional). If we later want the agent to push unsolicited
updates (e.g., "I noticed a new biomarker reading came in, want to adjust
the plan?"), we can upgrade to WebSocket then.

### New: Plan Designer (AI Chat + Manual Form)

The most significant new feature in the practitioner web app. A dedicated

- **AI Chat mode** (default for new plans) — conversational plan design

- **Manual mode** (fallback) — form-based plan builder.

Both modes read from and write to the **same plan draft** in the database.

#### AI Chat mode

A chat interface (similar to the existing per-patient AI chat at
`/patients/[username]/chat`, but powered by the Plan Designer agent
instead of the generic Q&A agent). The practitioner types in natural
language and the agent responds, making tool calls that modify the draft
in real-time.

**UI elements:**
- **Chat thread** — practitioner messages on the right, agent responses on
  the left. Tool-call events rendered as system messages ("AI added:
  Fasting Glucose, target < 100 mg/dL, daily morning").
- **Live draft preview** — a sidebar (or collapsible panel) showing the
  current draft state: title, outcome targets, metrics with targets,
  phases. Updates in real-time as the agent makes tool calls. This gives
  the practitioner a visual sense of the plan being built.
- **"Publish" button** — commits the draft to an active plan. Disabled
  until the draft has at least 1 metric and 1 outcome target.
- **"Switch to Manual" toggle** — switches to the form-based editor with
  the current draft pre-loaded.

#### Manual mode

The form-based plan builder. Same structure as the AI mode's live draft
preview, but interactive:

**UI flow:**
1. **Outcome targets section** — practitioner adds biomarker outcomes
   (selects from the patient's existing biomarker names or types a new one,
   sets target value + direction + date). Shows current value auto-synced
   from the biomarkers table.
2. **Tracked metrics section** — practitioner adds metrics from the template

3. **Phases section** — practitioner defines 1-3 phases (name, day range,

4. **Rationale field** — free-text explaining the plan's reasoning (visible

5. **Save draft** — saves to the DB draft (auto-saves on every change too).
6. **Publish** — commits the draft to an active plan (new version).
7. **"Switch to AI Chat" toggle** — switches to the chat interface with the

#### Draft lifecycle

1. **No draft, no active plan** (new patient): Practitioner opens the plan
   page. AI chat mode starts by default. Agent reads patient data and
   proposes a plan. Practitioner refines. Publishes.
2. **No draft, active plan exists** (editing): Practitioner opens the plan

   button. Clicking it creates a draft seeded from the active plan
   (`seed_from_active: true`). Practitioner edits in either mode. Publishes
   (creates a new version, archives the old one).
3. **Draft exists** (resuming): Practitioner opens the plan page. Sees the
   unpublished draft loaded in whichever mode they last used. Continues
   editing. Publishes or discards.

## 8. Voice agent (Bloom) changes

### Instruction update

Bloom's instruction (`backend/app/voice_agent/agent.py`) is updated to:

1. **Read the plan at session start.** Instead of the generic
   `get_today_progress`, Bloom calls a new `get_plan_progress` tool that
   returns the plan's metrics, today's adherence per metric, and outcome
   target progress. Bloom greets the patient with plan-specific context:
   "Good morning! You're on day 14 of your prediabetes plan. Your fasting
   glucose averaged 108 this week — a bit above your target of 100. But
   your steps are great, 9,200 average. How are you feeling today?"

2. **Log against plan metrics.** The `log_metric` tool takes a `metric_id`
   (which Bloom reads from the plan). Bloom maps natural-language utterances
   to the right metric: "I meditated for 15 minutes" →
   `meditation_minutes` metric; "I slept 6 hours" → `sleep_duration`;
   "My mood is a 3" → `mood`; "I took my morning meds" →
   `medication_adherence`. If multiple metrics could match, Bloom asks a
   quick clarifying question.

3. **Reference outcome targets in conversation.** When the patient asks
   "how am I doing?", Bloom references the outcome targets: "Your HbA1c
   was 6.1 at your last reading — your target is 6.0. You're close! Your
   fasting glucose is trending down too, which is a good sign."

4. **Surface insights conversationally.** Bloom can share correlations:
   "I noticed your mood is lower on days you sleep less than 6 hours. Want
   to talk about your sleep?" These come from the analytics endpoints.

5. **Suggest plan adjustments (to practitioner, not patient).** If Bloom
   notices a pattern (e.g., the patient consistently misses the sleep
   target), Bloom can say "I notice you've been hitting your step target
   but missing sleep — I'll let Dr. Sharma know she might want to adjust
   your plan." This creates a plan suggestion visible in the practitioner
   web app. Bloom does NOT change the plan itself.

### Tool changes (`backend/app/voice_agent/tools.py`)

| Old tool | New tool | Change |
|---|---|---|
| `log_wellness_entry(domain, value, note)` | `log_metric(metric_id, value, note, date)` | Generalized to any plan metric |
| `log_mood(score, note)` | `log_metric(metric_id, value, note)` | Mood is now just another metric (template: `mood`); the separate tool is removed |
| `get_today_progress` | `get_plan_progress` | Returns plan metrics + today's adherence + outcome progress |
| `get_streaks` | `get_streaks` | Updated to compute streaks per plan metric (not hardcoded domains) |
| `get_biomarker_trends` | `get_biomarker_trends` | Unchanged (biomarkers are separate from plan metrics) |
| (new) | `get_insights` | Returns recent AI insights (correlations, trend alerts) for Bloom to share conversationally |
| (new) | `suggest_plan_adjustment(reason, suggestion)` | Creates a plan suggestion visible to the practitioner. Bloom calls this when it notices a pattern. Does NOT change the plan. |

## 9. Migration strategy

### Goal

Migrate existing data (the demo patient + any real patients) from the
hardcoded 6-domain model to the new plan-based model without data loss.

### Steps

1. **Create the new tables** (`plans`, `plan_outcomes`, `plan_metrics`,
   `plan_phases`) — done in `_init_db_sync`, automatically on next startup.

2. **Add `metric_id` column to `daily_logs`** — done in `_init_db_sync`
   (using the existing `_column_exists` safe-migration pattern).

3. **For each existing patient with an onboarded profile:**
   a. Create a **default plan** (version 1, active) with:
      - Title: "Wellness Plan" (from the old `plan_json.summary`).
      - Rationale: the old `plan_json.summary` text.
      - Phases: from the old `plan_json.phases` (name, focus, actions,
        day_start/day_end computed as 1-30, 31-60, 61-90).
      - Metrics: one for each of the 6 old domains, using the closest
        template:
        - `workout` → `workout_minutes` (target: 30 min/day)
        - `diet` → `meals_logged` (target: 3/day)
        - `meditation` → `meditation_minutes` (target: 15 min/day)
        - `medication` → `medication_adherence` (target: 1 dose/day)
        - `mental_health` → `mood` (target: 3/5)
        - `other` → omitted (no good template match)
      - Outcomes: none (the old plan didn't have structured outcomes).
   b. **Re-point existing `daily_logs` rows:** for each row with a `domain`
      value, set `metric_id` to the corresponding metric in the new default
      plan. The `domain` column is kept (not dropped) for safety.

4. **Update all code** to read from plans + metric_id (new endpoints, new
   tools, new UI). The old `domain`-based code paths are removed.

5. **After verification**, a future cleanup can drop the `domain` column
   and the old `plan_json` field. Not done in this migration.

### When migration runs

The migration runs automatically on startup (in `_init_db_sync` or a
dedicated migration function called from `startup`). It checks: for each
onboarded patient with no active plan, create the default plan and
re-point logs. Idempotent — safe to run multiple times.

### Seed update

`seed.py` is updated so the demo patient gets a **practitioner-designed
plan** (created by the demo practitioner `dranya`) instead of the
auto-generated `plan_json`. This demonstrates the full feature: the plan
has outcome targets (HbA1c < 6.0, Vitamin D > 30), 5-6 tracked metrics
with targets, and 3 phases. The demo patient's existing 7 days of logs
are re-pointed to the new plan's metric IDs.

## 11. What is explicitly out of scope for this feature set

- **Free-form custom metrics** (practitioner defines a metric not in the
  template library). Future feature — the template library should cover
  ~95% of use cases for v1.
- **Wearable / Apple Health / Google Fit integration** (automatic data
  sync). All logging is manual (voice or tap) for now. Wearable integration
  is a natural follow-up.
- **Background insight engine** (continuous automated analysis). Insights
  are generated on-demand with caching for now.
- **Patient-edited plans.** The patient cannot edit their plan — only the
  practitioner can (see Decision 4).
- **Plan templates / reusable plans.** The practitioner designs each
  patient's plan individually. "Save this plan as a template for future
  patients" is a future feature.
- **Multi-practitioner co-management** (multiple practitioners editing the
  same patient's plan). The plan has a single `practitioner_id` author.
  Co-management is a future feature.
- **Historical plan comparison** (comparing adherence across plan versions).
  Old plans are archived (not deleted) so this is possible in the future,
  but no UI for it in this feature set.
- **Real-time notifications when the plan changes.** The patient sees the
  updated plan on next dashboard refresh. Push notifications are a separate
  future feature.

## 12. Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Plan** | A patient-specific tracking plan designed by a practitioner (or auto-generated by Bloom as a default). Contains outcome targets, tracked metrics, and phases. |
| **Plan draft** | A work-in-progress plan that hasn't been published yet. Both the AI chat mode and the manual mode edit the same draft. When the practitioner clicks "Publish", the draft becomes a new active plan version. |
| **Outcome target** | A biomarker goal (e.g., HbA1c < 6.0%). The *why* of the plan. Measured infrequently via labs. |
| **Tracked metric** (or **behavioral lever**) | A frequently-logged metric (e.g., daily steps, sleep hours). The *how* of the plan. Has a target the patient aims for. |
| **Metric template** | A pre-defined metric type in the `METRIC_TEMPLATES` library (e.g., "fasting_glucose", "steps"). Defines the unit, frequency options, target type, default target, and visualization. |
| **Adherence** | The percentage of a metric's target that the patient achieved, per the computation rules in section 5. |
| **Phase** | A segment of the plan (e.g., days 1-30) with a focus and a set of emphasized metrics. |
| **Plan suggestion** | An AI-proposed adjustment to the plan, surfaced to the practitioner for approval. Not auto-applied. |
| **Plan Designer** | The ADK agent that powers AI-assisted plan creation/editing in chat mode. Reads patient data, proposes metrics/targets, modifies the draft via tools, explains clinical reasoning. |
| **BFF** | Backend-for-Frontend. The Next.js practitioner app's auth pattern: httpOnly cookie on the Next.js origin, bearer token forwarded server-side to FastAPI. |
| **SSE** | Server-Sent Events. Used for streaming the Plan Designer agent's responses from the backend to the web app. Unidirectional server→client streaming. |
